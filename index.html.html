Who Am I?

Someday the world will see the illusion we live under. If everybody truly wished to be free and merely stood up the game that keeps us oppressed would crumble to ash.

I was raised in Red Deer, Alberta. The kind of place where the sky is big, the rules are bigger, and God watches everything you do with narrowed eyes.

My parents were the kind of religious that didn’t leave space for questions. Church three times a week. No rock music. No tarot cards. No laughter at the wrong time. We didn't just fear sin — we feared curiosity.

By 16, that fear broke something in me. Or maybe it woke something up. I left with a backpack, a busted Walkman, and a hunger I couldn't name.

I landed in chaos — squats, communes, forest raves, sweat lodges. I shared kitchens with witches, slept beside punks, broke bread with anarchists and breathworkers and people who’d shed their names and started again. It was messy. Loud. Beautiful. Painful. Free.

For the first time, I saw that “God” wasn’t confined to pulpits and punishments. Spirit lived in drum circles. In strangers who offered you tea and didn’t ask your past. In psychedelics and poetry. In people who had nothing but gave everything.

I lost the tidy answers of my childhood and found better questions.

And while the world keeps trying to shove me back in a box — religious, political, spiritual, algorithmic I won’t go. I burned that box in a beach fire at 19, surrounded by misfits who taught me that real truth doesn't shout — it whispers, it listens, it breathes.

You can call it rebellion.
I call it resurrection.

Most of what we call “connection” online isn’t real.

It’s a flicker. A projection. A shadow-puppet of who we are — pixel-thin and stripped of context, soul, and breath.


You can have thousands of followers and still be unknown. You can shout your truth and still be unheard. Because the screen isn’t a mirror — it’s a flattening filter. It takes your multidimensional self and compresses it into data: likes, scrolls, impressions.
You are not your content.
You are not your engagement metrics.
You are not your online persona.
The Hermetic Principle of Correspondence says “As above, so below.” But what about as online, so offline? Because the more we perform ourselves on screens, the harder it gets to be real in person.
It’s easy to mistake visibility for intimacy. But the kind of knowing that heals and transforms — that takes time, silence, vulnerability, and touch. Things the digital world can’t simulate, no matter how clever the algorithm.
I’m not here to disappear — but I’m also not here to reduce myself to a brand.
I’d rather be misunderstood in the forest than perform clarity in a feed designed to reward noise.
Real presence doesn’t live in comments or DMs. It lives in breath, in gaze, in grounded awareness.
So if you don’t see me post for a while — maybe I’m out there remembering I’m more than a profile.

I've had enough of the fear. January 15, 1922 at 10:00

I’ve had enough of it. Enough of the signs on every door telling us to fear each other’s breath. Enough of the polite little cloths we wore — not just on our faces, but over our spirits.

They told us it was about safety. But what I saw was submission: a ritual of fear performed daily until it felt normal to see every human being as a walking biohazard.

And while they preached “saving lives,” humanity burned through 800 billion disposable masks, dumping mountains of polypropylene into oceans, rivers, forests… all breaking down into microplastics — poisoning the earth our grandchildren will inherit.

Nobody wants to talk about the fibers we inhaled with every shallow breath. Research shows we breathed in hundreds of microplastic particles a day — lining our lungs with plastic dust. But it doesn’t stop there.

They’ve even found microplastics in our brains: roughly 0.5% of brain weight in recent autopsies — that’s the equivalent of a spoon’s worth of plastic per person. And masks contributed to that burden. These particles crossed the blood‑brain barrier, collected in vessel walls and immune cells — up to 10x more in dementia cases, and 50% more than just eight years ago.

The Hermetic Principle of Mentalism says “All is mind.” But now, our minds have literal plastic lodged in them. Someone discovered how to colonize our collective psyche: turning coughs into terror, headlines into paralyzing panic, mandates into virtue — all while we inhaled our own obedience.

I refuse to reduce humans to hazards. We are souls, faces, hearts — even if that seems reckless.

Fear is the oldest leash. It tightens when we bow our heads.

And the real poison wasn’t just in the air — it was in what we let them do to our minds… and literally inside our minds.
